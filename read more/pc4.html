Author: Adam Simmons
Date published: May 13th 2016

Up until now, Samsung's 27 curved models have featured only a subtle 4000R curve. As noted in our review of the S27E650C, it's so subtle that it's barely noticeable and at any rate doesn't really bring much to the gaming or overall viewing experience. The Samsung C27F591FD (various regional suffixes may be used) of the CF591 series is here to set things straight or should that be, make things less straight? With a curvature of 1800R, the curve of this model is over twice as steep as that on previous models. This is designed to offer a more engrossing experience and make the user feel more immersed into their content. A key attraction is that the bezels are very thin, with a slender panel border and outer component. There is also a mixture of white and metallic-looking silver going on elsewhere which is a little different to most monitors out there. We feel it looks unique without looking unusual or tacky, but everyone has their own preferences on that. The OSD (On Screen Display) is again controlled by a joystick (JOG button) at the rear of the monitor, towards the right when looking from the front.

The panel used is a 27" Samsung curved SVA (Multi-Domain Vertical Alignment) with 1800R curvature, 1920 x 1080 (Full HD) resolution and 3000:1 static contrast. We suspect this will use a smooth and light matte screen surface and will employ true 8-bit colour. There are a few more tricks up this monitor's sleeves as well. It has a 60Hz refresh rate, but this is variable with Adaptive-Sync. The monitor therefore supports AMD FreeSync for compatible GPUs, although the variable refresh rate range is currently unknown. A flicker-free backlight is used, with a specified 81% NTSC (119.3% sRGB) colour gamut coverage. Samsung states in one of their more recent press releases that 'active color crystal technology' is used, so possibly a Quantum Dot based backlight. This is interesting because that puts it well beyond the 72% NTSC coverage you typically see on Full HD models. Other aspects of the specification to note are a 4ms specified response time (not to be taken at face value) and a 250 cd/m2 typical luminance. The 'Eye Saver' Low Blue Light (LBL) setting will be making a comeback and so will the less than welcome 'Game Mode'. The former setting is actually quite useful for viewing comfort as it massively reduces blue light emission from the monitor (particularly good before bed) and also minimises contrast to try to minimise the amount of time the eye spends adapting to lighting changes. An alternative LBL setting in the form of 'Warm2' will likely feature, as with other Samsung models, as well. The rear of the monitor is glossy and white, devoid of VESA holes (you're stuck with the tilt-only stand) and featuring some rear-facing ports; DP 1.2, HDMI 1.4a (with Adaptive-Sync), a 3.5mm headphone jack and a further digital input plus 2 x 5W speakers. The full capabilities of the monitor (including FreeSync) can be harnessed over HDMI only. AMD's FreeSync information page notes that FreeSync is available via DP (but not HDMI) on this model, which is incorrect. At any rate, a FreeSync range of 48 72Hz is specified which is achieved on the monitor with the 'Ultimate Engine' option selected.
